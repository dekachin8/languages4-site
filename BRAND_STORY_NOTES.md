# Brand Story Notes: "What ARE Languages 4?"
*Raw material for a potential relaunch article, captured April 17, 2026, from the hero redesign conversation.*

## Why these notes exist

Tim flagged this mid-conversation: the creative back-and-forth on how to surface the L4 brand concept in the new hero section is itself article-worthy for the site relaunch. These notes capture the core concept, the design decisions, and direct quotes that could seed a longer piece.

---

## The core brand concept (one paragraph)

**"Languages 4" is a question disguised as a name.**

Read aloud, it sounds like "Languages for" — and that's deliberate. The "4" is both a numeral and a homophone, and it answers two questions at once: *what is the company called?* and *what are languages for?* The answer has lived in the logo from the beginning: **Reclamation. Revitalization. Preservation. Communication.** Four purposes. Four letters after the word. Full circle — literally, because the logo is L4 in the center of a ring carrying those four words.

---

## The four purposes, defined (why they matter)

These aren't synonyms — they're distinct methodologies recognized across the Indigenous language field:

- **Reclamation** — bringing a dormant or "sleeping" language back from documented records, often generations after the last fluent speaker.
- **Revitalization** — strengthening a language that's still spoken but endangered, growing its daily use and speaker base.
- **Preservation** — documenting, recording, and protecting language materials for future access.
- **Communication** — the ongoing use of the language in real life: between generations, across distances, and in new domains (digital, ceremonial, commercial).

Each community's work lives in a different mix of these modes. A single brand that names all four signals: *we meet you where your language is, not where we wish it was*.

## What's notably absent: Representation

Worth naming. The existing `/whatarel4` hero carries a fifth word: Representation. It was considered for the new hero and deliberately omitted — not because it doesn't matter, but because it's expressed elsewhere in the brand voice.

> **Tim's exact framing**: *"I like Representation, but I think that is 'represented' by 'community-led and sovereignty.'"*

Representation isn't a separate deliverable — it's a *condition* of the work. It shows up in who leads the projects, who owns the materials, whose voices are in the content. The hero subhead carries that explicitly: *"community-led, grounded in language sovereignty."*

## The logo as argument

The brand identity has always been right: a circle with **L4** in the center, surrounded by the four words. The hero has finally caught up to the logo. The new hero structure — *question → wordmark → four purposes* — is the logo unrolled linearly, preserving the "full circle" logic while working in a typography-first format.

The logo answers the question silently.
The hero answers it with words.
Both say the same thing.

## Design constraints that shaped the current hero

Captured here because the article might explore the design journey:

1. **The "4" needed to carry weight.** Previously the wordmark was just "Languages 4" with no visible connection to the four purposes. The pun was dead on arrival.

2. **Four long R-words are typographically heavy.** Earlier attempts to include them as a tagline failed because they overpowered the wordmark or wrapped messily on mobile.

3. **SEO pulled in one direction, brand in another.** The audit said "Indigenous Language Reclamation" should be the keyword-rich H1. The brand said the name itself needed to lead. The final H1 does both: it's one semantic H1 containing all three elements (the question, the wordmark, the four purposes).

4. **Mobile reality.** A 2×2 grid for the four words reinforces the "4" count visually while solving the mobile wrapping problem — four quadrants, four purposes.

## Copy that emerged

Final hero copy, as shipped:

> **What are Languages 4?**
> **Languages 4**
> Reclamation · Revitalization · Preservation · Communication
> 
> Custom software and land-based curriculum, built with Indigenous communities.
> 
> Long-term partnerships — community-led, grounded in language sovereignty.
> 
> **[Start a conversation]**
> *30-minute call · platform walkthrough · bring your questions*

Every word was weighed. Worth noting the CTA change from "Schedule Your Free Demonstration" (SaaS-demo energy) to "Start a conversation" (partnership energy) — deliberate recognition that tribal leadership and language program directors aren't impulse SaaS buyers. The button support phrase "bring your questions" is unusual for B2B and specifically models the humility the brand claims.

## Article angle options

### Angle 1: "What ARE languages 4?" — the brand explained
A first-person piece from Tim. Opens with the question, walks through the four purposes, closes with the logo-as-argument point. Natural relaunch piece. Personal voice.

### Angle 2: "Four words, one mission"
Each of the four R-words gets a dedicated section: what it means in practice, who's doing it, a community example from the L4 portfolio. Serves both as brand explainer AND as an SEO hub linking to relevant signature-collections articles.

### Angle 3: "How we redesigned our home page to honor our logo"
The design-decision story itself. Meta, slightly insidery — but the kind of thing that builds trust with sophisticated readers (and signals to potential community partners that L4 thinks carefully before it ships). Could draw traffic from design/brand/marketing readers who arrive unrelated to the product, then learn about it.

### Angle 4: "Why Languages 4 isn't Indigenous Language Reclamation alone"
The Representation absence as the hook. The argument that the word Representation earns its place through *how* you work, not *what* you claim. Tim's own framing ("represented by community-led and sovereignty") becomes the thesis.

My instinct: **Angle 1** for the relaunch (it's the strongest introduction for a first-time visitor), with **Angle 2** as a follow-up pillar piece that can sustain the content-hub pivot. Angle 3 is a nice "inside the build" piece for month 2. Angle 4 is a strong essay for the pillar-content hub later.

## For the article writer (Tim or otherwise)

- This isn't a retrospective brand story — it's the *first* public telling of the concept. The relaunch is the reveal.
- The hero itself is the shortest version of the story. The article is the long version.
- The tone Tim uses in conversation (direct, a little self-deprecating, willing to say "that was hard to fit in a visually appealing form") is the tone to keep. Don't sanitize it.
- The piece should end with an invitation — the same CTA as the hero ("Start a conversation") or a softer version of it.
